Use of Cartograms

What are they for?

A cartogram is a type of graphic that changes the area of a region in a map depending on a certain statistic.

Because it does not depict geographic space but rather changes the size of regions depending on a certain statistic, a cartogram is not a true map.

They vary on their degree in which geographic space is changed; some appear very similar to a real-life map, others do not.

There are four main types of cartograms, each have different ways of displaying a certain statistic (Contiguous, Non-contiguous, Dorling and Rectangular).

The type of cartogram that this software produces is Contiguous cartograms.

Their Main Uses

They are mainly used to portray statistical representations of particular regions.

They are particularly good for showing things like numbers (of people, things - whether man-made or natural - or quantities) and a classic example is voting maps like "number of votes for party X for each constituency". Maps such as a chloropleth map that use real-world geography are misleading because they show number of votes but not the actual population density in each area; a cartogram does just that.
